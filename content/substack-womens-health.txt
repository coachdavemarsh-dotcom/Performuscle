TITLE: Why Your Training Is Not Working the Way It Should

SUBTITLE: The physiological reality of being a woman in sport that most coaches never explain

---

This comes up constantly with the women I coach. They are working hard. They are consistent. They are doing most things right by any reasonable standard. But their results do not match their effort. Their weight fluctuates by five to ten pounds in a week for no apparent reason. Their energy and performance shift dramatically across the month. Fat loss has stalled despite a deficit they can prove on paper.

The most common explanation they have been given is some version of "try harder" or "track more carefully."

That is not the answer. The answer is physiology. Specifically, the physiology they were never taught.

---

THE FUNDAMENTAL PROBLEM

Almost everything we know about training and nutrition was learned by studying men. The research base is improving, but slowly. The practical result is that most coaching frameworks, most programs, and most advice you will read online was built for a male hormonal environment and then applied to everyone.

Men are essentially the same every day. Their hormonal environment is relatively stable, which means their response to training, their fuel usage, their hunger, their recovery capacity, and their body weight are all reasonably predictable from day to day.

Women are not. During the menstrual cycle, insulin sensitivity changes. Fuel preference shifts. Metabolic rate moves. Hunger fluctuates by hundreds of calories per day. Coordination, strength, and injury risk all vary. Body weight can shift five to ten pounds in a single week from water retention alone, driven entirely by hormonal changes and having nothing to do with actual fat gain.

Treating a woman's training like a man's training with a smaller calorie number is not just incomplete. It is working against the physiology rather than with it.

---

THE TWO PHASES, AND WHY THEY MATTER

Your menstrual cycle, averaging around 28 days, divides into two fundamentally different physiological environments.

The first half is the follicular phase. Estrogen is dominant. This is actually your performance-friendly hormone. It improves how well your muscles use carbohydrates for fuel, promotes fat burning, limits the damage done by hard training, and speeds up muscle repair between sessions. Research suggests women make their best strength and muscle gains when their hardest training lands in this estrogen-dominant phase.

The second half is the luteal phase. Progesterone takes over. Progesterone is a different story. It raises your resting core temperature by about half a degree, which increases perceived effort and impairs temperature regulation during exercise. It drives insulin resistance, making your muscles less efficient at using carbohydrates. It activates fat-storage enzymes, particularly in the lower body. And it drives hunger up by anywhere from 90 to 500 calories per day above your normal baseline.

The luteal phase is not your training enemy. But it requires a different approach than the follicular phase. Women who try to run the exact same training and nutrition plan for the full month are fighting their physiology for two weeks out of every four.

---

WHY LOWER BODY FAT IS NOT A WILLPOWER PROBLEM

This one matters enormously, because I see it cause unnecessary distress in almost every female client I have worked with.

Fat cells in different parts of the body have different receptor ratios. Two key receptors control how readily fat is released for burning: beta-2 receptors promote release, alpha-2 receptors block it. Lower body fat in women, specifically hips, thighs, and glutes, has a much higher ratio of alpha-2 to beta-2 receptors than upper body fat. This means the hormonal signals that normally mobilize fat, particularly adrenaline during exercise and fasting, are actively blocked in those areas.

Estrogen directly amplifies this effect. It upregulates the alpha-2 receptors in lower body fat tissue and activates fat-storage enzymes in those same areas. Progesterone adds another layer on top of that in the luteal phase.

Lower body fat is not stubborn because of what you are eating or how hard you are training. It is stubborn because it is biologically designed to be. It is an evolutionary reserve for pregnancy and lactation, and your hormonal environment is actively protecting it. Women who understand this stop wasting energy on approaches that are not going to change the underlying physiology, and start working with it instead.

---

ENERGY AVAILABILITY: THE CONCEPT EVERY FEMALE ATHLETE NEEDS TO UNDERSTAND

Energy availability refers to how much dietary energy is left over for normal body functions after accounting for the energy you spend in exercise. Drop below the critical threshold and the body begins shutting down non-essential systems to survive.

The threshold is approximately 30 calories per kilogram of lean body mass per day.

Below that level, the menstrual cycle is one of the first things to go. And this is where I want to be very clear, because this belief has caused real damage in athletic populations: losing your period is not a normal adaptation to training. It is a warning sign that something has gone wrong.

Amenorrhea, the loss of the menstrual cycle, is driven by chronically low energy availability, not by low body fat percentage. Women with normal body fat can lose their periods if they are training hard enough and not eating enough to cover it. And the consequences go well beyond fertility.

The estrogen drop that accompanies amenorrhea accelerates bone density loss. Studies have found that amenorrheic athletes can have the bone density of women 20 to 30 years older than their actual age. This is not something that bounces back quickly when eating resumes. And going on hormonal birth control does not fix it. Birth control can restore the appearance of a cycle, but it does not restore the estrogen levels that protect bone density when the underlying energy deficit is still there.

If you are experiencing irregular or absent periods, persistent fatigue that does not respond to rest, stress fractures, or difficulty maintaining muscle despite consistent training, energy availability is the first thing I would investigate.

The fix is straightforward: add approximately 250 to 360 calories per day to your current intake and reduce training by one session per week. It sounds counterintuitive when you are trying to lose fat. But a body in hormonal distress is not going to produce results until the foundation is repaired.

---

STRESS IS A TRAINING VARIABLE

Most coaches treat psychological stress as a lifestyle issue, separate from training. It is not. Stress directly affects the hormonal environment that determines how your training produces results.

Women and men respond to stress differently at the hormonal level. Women release cortisol more rapidly in response to a stressor, reach higher peak cortisol levels, and clear it more slowly. The practical consequence is that women are more susceptible to the chronic cortisol elevation that comes from persistent work pressure, relationship stress, poor sleep, or any combination of life circumstances.

Chronically elevated cortisol causes the kidneys to retain sodium and water. This shows up as persistent puffiness that can mask fat loss completely on the scale for weeks at a time. It increases insulin resistance, promotes abdominal fat storage, suppresses the hormonal signaling that drives the menstrual cycle, breaks down muscle protein, and disrupts sleep, which then elevates the next day's cortisol.

The combined effect of high training stress plus high life stress plus a caloric deficit can push a woman into a state where fat loss stops entirely, despite a deficit that looks correct on paper. The body is in a state of perceived emergency and has decided that losing fat is not a priority right now.

If you are doing everything right on paper but getting nowhere, and you are also sleeping poorly, feeling unusually irritable, retaining water persistently, and your cycle has gone irregular, that is not a calorie tracking problem. That is a cortisol problem.

---

WHAT TO ACTUALLY DO

Here is the short version of the practical framework I use with my female clients.

Train with your cycle, not against it. In the follicular phase, push your hardest sessions, attempt personal bests, add training days if needed. In the luteal phase, maintain rather than progress. Keep intensity manageable and accept that some sessions will feel harder than they should. The performance dip is real and physiologically explained. Fighting it aggressively tends to increase injury risk without improving results.

Start your diet in the follicular phase. When you begin a caloric deficit matters. Starting in the follicular phase, when insulin sensitivity is better and hunger is closer to normal, sets you up for two good weeks before the harder luteal weeks arrive. Starting in the luteal phase is starting in the worst possible window.

Eat slightly more in the luteal phase. Strictly maintaining a fixed calorie target across the entire month ignores the legitimate changes in metabolic rate and hunger that occur in the luteal phase. Adding 100 to 200 calories in the second half of your cycle improves adherence across the full month without meaningfully slowing fat loss over time.

Lose fat slowly. Slower fat loss rates produce proportionally more actual fat lost and less lean mass lost than aggressive restriction. For women, the sustainable deficit is smaller than it is for men, and pushing past it is more likely to trigger hormonal disruption. A monthly loss of 0.5 to 1% of body weight is almost always the better approach.

Treat stress as a training variable. When life stress is high, productive training capacity goes down. Trying to maintain full volume during genuinely stressful life periods usually produces worse results than backing off slightly and protecting sleep and recovery quality.

---

THE BIGGER POINT

Women's physiology is genuinely more complex than men's to train around. Not because women are fragile. Because there are more variables in play, and those variables shift across the month.

But that complexity comes with tools that most women never get shown. Understanding your cycle gives you information that translates directly into better decisions about when to push, when to back off, when to eat more, and how to read what your body is telling you.

That is not making things more complicated. That is finally working with an accurate model of how your body actually operates.
