TITLE: Your Birth Control Might Be Holding Back Your Training

SUBTITLE: The conversation your doctor and your coach are probably not having

---

This comes up in almost every female client intake call I do. Someone mentions they have been on the pill for years, or they recently switched to a new form of birth control, and they cannot figure out why their training has gone sideways. Their energy is flat. Recovery is slower than it should be. Progress has stalled.

And when they asked their ob/gyn about it, they were reassured everything was fine.

I want to be clear here: I am not anti-birth control. It exists for important reasons. But I have noticed that the vast majority of active women are making decisions about hormonal birth control without anyone explaining what it actually does to the physiology that drives training.

That gap has always bothered me. So here is what I know.

---

YOUR HORMONES AND TRAINING

Before we talk about birth control, you need a basic picture of what your hormones are doing across your monthly cycle, because that context is everything.

The first half of the cycle, the follicular phase, is dominated by estrogen. Estrogen is actually your training-friendly hormone. It improves insulin sensitivity, promotes fat burning during exercise, supports muscle repair, reduces inflammation after hard sessions, and has direct effects on how your skeletal muscle remodels after training. Research suggests women make their best strength and hypertrophy gains when their hardest sessions land in this estrogen-dominant phase.

The second half, the luteal phase, is dominated by progesterone. Progesterone works against you in the gym. It increases insulin resistance, raises your resting body temperature, blocks many of the positive effects estrogen would otherwise have, and drives hunger up significantly, often by several hundred calories per day. It also activates the fat-storage enzymes responsible for that lower-body fat women tend to struggle with most.

And then there is testosterone. Women produce far less of it than men, but the amounts within the normal female range have real consequences for strength, power, muscle gain, aerobic capacity, and the drive to train hard in the first place. Female athletes with higher testosterone levels within the normal range consistently outperform those with lower levels on measures of strength, power, and adaptation to training.

This matters because here is what hormonal birth control does: it reduces testosterone.

---

WHAT BIRTH CONTROL ACTUALLY DOES

Almost all hormonal birth control works by supplying synthetic hormones to your body, which signals your brain that everything is already taken care of. The brain responds by cutting production of the hormones that drive ovulation, and as a side effect, your natural estrogen and progesterone drop significantly.

Combined birth control replaces the reduced estrogen with a synthetic form called ethinyl estradiol, and the reduced progesterone with a synthetic compound called a progestin. Progestin-only methods do not replace the estrogen at all. That missing estrogen matters for muscle repair, fat metabolism, and recovery.

But the biggest issue for active women is testosterone.

Birth control reduces testosterone through two routes. First, it cuts production by suppressing the signals your ovaries and adrenal glands receive to produce testosterone. A 2014 systematic review and meta-analysis published in Human Reproduction Update, pooling data across more than 8,000 women, found that combined oral contraceptives reduce total and free testosterone by around 50% on average (Zimmermann et al., 2014). Second, the synthetic estrogen in combined birth control raises levels of a binding protein called SHBG, which grabs free testosterone and makes it unavailable to your muscles. Less free testosterone means a weaker anabolic signal, lower training drive, and for some women, lower libido.

The delivery method matters more than most people realise. Research comparing the three main combined contraceptive formats found that the vaginal ring raises SHBG by 348%, the transdermal patch by 247%, and a standard combined oral pill by around 57% (van den Heuvel et al., 2005). These are meaningfully different numbers with meaningfully different impacts on your free testosterone.

---

NOT ALL BIRTH CONTROL IS THE SAME

This is where most conversations stop. But the type of birth control you are on matters enormously.

The progestin generation matters. First and second generation progestins have androgenic activity, meaning they interact with the testosterone receptor. Second generation progestins like levonorgestrel are the most androgenic. Third generation progestins like norgestimate are less androgenic. Fourth generation progestins like drospirenone, found in Yaz and Yasmin, are anti-androgenic. They actively block the androgen receptor.

For general health, anti-androgenic progestins can be great. They reduce acne, limit bloating, and can cause a small amount of water weight loss. But for a woman who is strength training, having the androgen receptor blocked by a compound that does not send a positive muscle-building signal could mean significantly limited gains. This is the progestin generation least suited to women who care about building muscle.

Triphasic versus monophasic also matters. Triphasic pills vary their hormone dose across three different phases of the pill cycle, including a high-progestin third week. Multiple studies have found that triphasic pills reduce VO2 max, increase fat gain, and impair high-intensity performance in ways that monophasic pills, which maintain constant hormone levels, do not (Lebrun, 1993; Casazza et al., 2002). If you are on the pill, knowing whether it is monophasic or triphasic is worth a five-minute conversation with your doctor.

Depo-Provera is in its own category. If you care about body composition, bone density, or training performance, the evidence against it is clear and consistent. A long-term prospective study found an average of 11 pounds of weight gain over three years, 9 of which were fat, and a doubled risk of becoming obese compared to women using oral contraceptives (Berenson and Rahman, 2009). It also has the worst effects on bone mineral density of any hormonal contraceptive. I do not recommend it to active women.

---

THE BRIGHT SPOTS

None of this means birth control is bad. The benefits for active women are real.

Birth control can reduce monthly blood loss by up to 50%, which meaningfully lowers the risk of iron deficiency anemia, already disproportionately common in female athletes (Iyer et al., 2000). Women who experience significant PMS, PMDD, or large performance swings across their cycle can use birth control to flatten those fluctuations and train more consistently across the month. Twelve weeks of compromised training per year because of a brutal luteal phase is a serious problem that birth control can solve. A 2017 systematic review and meta-analysis also found that oral contraceptive use is associated with a reduction in ACL injury risk of approximately 20%, by eliminating the estrogen spike before ovulation that temporarily weakens ligament and tendon strength (Herzberg et al., 2017).

The decision is always about weighing your specific situation. I am not here to push anyone off birth control. I am here to give you enough information to make the decision yourself.

---

WHAT I TELL MY CLIENTS

Here is the framework I use with the women I coach.

If performance and body composition are priorities and you have flexibility in your choice, the hormonal IUD stands apart from everything else. Its effects are almost entirely local to the uterus. It does not raise SHBG, does not reduce your testosterone, does not affect VO2 max or training adaptations. From a pure performance standpoint, it is the best option on the market.

If you want an oral pill, a monophasic version with a second or third generation progestin is a considerably better choice than a triphasic pill. Ask your doctor specifically. The distinction is not always volunteered.

If you are on progestin-only birth control, adding 25 to 30 grams of soy protein or 50 to 100 milligrams of isolated phytoestrogens daily may partially replace the estrogen signaling you have lost. Neither is on any banned substance list.

If your training has gone backwards since starting or changing birth control, get a hormone panel. Not to come off birth control necessarily, but to understand the environment you are training in and adjust your approach accordingly.

---

THE BIGGER POINT

Active women deserve to have this information. The fact that most gynecologists are not exercise physiologists, and most coaches are not gynecologists, has created a gap where millions of women are making significant decisions about their health and their training without anyone connecting the dots.

That changes when you know the right questions to ask.

If you found this useful, share it with someone who needs it. And if you want to work with a coach who builds your programming around how your physiology actually works, rather than ignoring it, that is exactly what I do.
