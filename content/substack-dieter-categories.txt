TITLE: The Reason Your Diet Isn't Working Has Nothing to Do With Willpower

SUBTITLE: Your body fat percentage determines the rules of the game. Most people are playing by the wrong ones.

---

Here is something I see constantly with new clients.

Someone has been dieting for months. They are tracking their food. They are training consistently. They are doing what they have been told works. And either nothing is happening, or something is happening but it feels completely unsustainable and they are falling apart physically and mentally trying to maintain it.

The most common problem is not a lack of discipline. It is not a broken metabolism. It is not even bad information, necessarily.

It is that they are using the wrong set of rules for where they currently are.

The way your body responds to a caloric deficit, how fast you can safely lose fat, how large a deficit you can sustain, and what risks you face when you push too hard are all determined by one number more than any other. Your body fat percentage.

This is the foundation of what I call the 3-Category Dieter Framework.

---

WHY ONE APPROACH CANNOT WORK FOR EVERYONE

The standard dieting advice most people receive sounds like this: eat 500 calories less than you burn, lose roughly half a kilogram per week, repeat until done.

That advice was designed with a specific type of person in mind. Someone carrying a significant amount of excess body fat. Someone for whom a 500 calorie deficit is genuinely manageable because their fat stores are large enough to comfortably cover it.

Apply that same approach to an already lean athlete trying to get leaner and you will not just get poor results. You will cause hormonal disruption, muscle loss, and physical deterioration. You will be asking the body to create a deficit it does not have the fat reserves to support.

Apply it in the other direction and you have the opposite problem. A person carrying 35% body fat could safely lose at a faster rate, reduce more aggressively, and recover far more quickly from periods of restriction. Telling them to lose half a kilogram per week is not cautious. It is unnecessarily slow.

The framework exists because bodies at different levels of leanness operate by genuinely different physiological rules. Getting the category wrong does not just slow progress. It causes problems.

---

THE THREE CATEGORIES

CATEGORY 3 — HIGHER BODY FAT (typically above 27 to 28% for women, above 20 to 22% for men)

Category 3 is where most people start. Body fat stores are substantial, which creates a significant physiological advantage for dieting.

When there is plenty of stored fat available, the body does not panic in a deficit. It releases fat readily because it correctly registers that reserves are not under threat. Leptin levels, the hormone responsible for signalling satiety and keeping metabolic rate up, start from a higher baseline. Hormonal systems are robust. The machinery of fat loss works the way it is supposed to.

This is why general dietary advice works reasonably well for Category 3 individuals. A 400 to 600 calorie daily deficit produces consistent fat loss without serious side effects. Rates of loss of 0.75 to 1 kilogram per week are achievable and sustainable. Performance in the gym stays largely intact because there is ample energy available even in a deficit.

The other thing worth knowing about Category 3 is that the health gains come quickly. Improved blood sugar regulation, better sleep, reduced inflammation, improved cardiovascular markers often show up before any dramatic physical change. The body is highly responsive at this stage.

As fat loss progresses and body fat drops toward the Category 2 range, the rules of the game begin to change. This is a transition point many coaches miss. What worked at 35% will not work the same way at 25%, and the approach needs to shift accordingly.

---

CATEGORY 2 — MODERATE BODY FAT (typically 20 to 27% for women, 13 to 20% for men)

Category 2 is where most recreational exercisers and intermediate fitness enthusiasts sit. This is also the most common starting point for structured physique and performance coaching.

At Category 2 body fat levels, the physiology becomes more nuanced. Fat is still available in reasonable quantities and the body will release it, but it is starting to become more conservative. The dramatic early responsiveness of Category 3 is behind you. Progress is still very achievable, but it requires more precision.

Deficits of 300 to 500 calories per day work well. Rates of loss of 0.5 to 0.75% of body weight per week are appropriate targets. Protein needs to be higher than general population recommendations, sitting around 2.0 to 2.3 grams per kilogram of body weight per day to protect lean tissue while in a deficit.

Category 2 dieters typically sustain 8 to 16 week dieting phases before needing a structured break at maintenance. Hormonal systems are generally resilient at this level. Menstrual cycle disruption is uncommon if the deficit is appropriate. Training performance stays largely intact with the right approach.

One tool that works particularly well at Category 2 is carbohydrate cycling. Higher carbohydrate intake on training days supports performance and muscle retention. Lower carbohydrate on rest days creates the weekly caloric deficit without compromising the sessions that drive adaptation. The body at this body fat level is responsive to carbohydrate-driven insulin signalling in a way that makes cycling genuinely effective.

---

CATEGORY 1 — VERY LEAN (typically below 17 to 18% for women, below 10 to 12% for men)

This is where the rules change completely.

Category 1 dieters are already genuinely lean. They are athletes, competitive physique athletes, or people who have been consistently training and dieting and are now in the final stages of a fat-loss phase. They are trying to get leaner still, and the body is fighting them at every step.

Here is what happens physiologically at low body fat. As fat stores drop, the body begins registering that its reserves are getting low. It starts protecting them. Fat mobilisation becomes harder, not easier. Leptin drops significantly, which increases hunger and reduces metabolic rate simultaneously. Cortisol rises, which promotes muscle breakdown and makes remaining fat harder to release. Thyroid output decreases. And in women, the hormonal axis that controls the menstrual cycle begins to downregulate because reproduction is energetically expensive and the body treats a low-energy state as an emergency.

None of this is a mental problem. It is biology responding exactly as it evolved to respond.

The practical implications are significant.

The maximum safe rate of fat loss for a Category 1 dieter is approximately 200 to 350 grams per week. Attempting to lose faster than this at very low body fat means you are losing muscle, not fat, because the fat stores simply cannot support a larger deficit.

The daily caloric deficit needs to be small. Typically 200 to 300 calories. This is not timidity. It is a physiological constraint. Push the deficit larger and you are not dieting more effectively. You are creating the conditions for muscle loss, hormonal disruption, and the clinical syndrome called RED-S.

Protein needs are higher than any other category, around 2.3 to 2.5 grams per kilogram of body weight per day at minimum, because the catabolic environment of low body fat dieting puts lean tissue at serious risk.

Diet breaks are not optional. Every four to six weeks of active dieting, a one to two week period at maintenance is required. This allows leptin to partially recover, cortisol to drop, and the metabolic adaptations that accumulate during a deficit to partially reset. Skipping this step does not make the diet more efficient. It makes it progressively less effective and eventually causes it to stall completely.

And dieting phases should be short. Eight to twelve weeks maximum before a break. Category 1 is not a state where you can sustain a long continuous cut. The body will not allow it without serious consequences.

---

THE COST OF GETTING IT WRONG

Using the wrong category rules in either direction causes real problems.

A Category 3 person approaching their diet with Category 1 caution, tiny deficits and very slow rates of loss, is leaving significant results on the table. They have the physiological capacity to push harder and they are not using it.

A Category 1 person approaching their diet with Category 3 aggression, large deficits and fast rates of loss, is heading toward hormonal disruption, muscle loss, and potentially serious health consequences. Their body does not have the fat stores to support what they are asking of it.

The framework is not about making dieting complicated. It is about matching the approach to the actual physiology. When you do that, the right strategy becomes simpler, not harder, because you stop fighting against your own biology and start working with it.

---

HOW TO KNOW WHICH CATEGORY YOU ARE IN

Body fat percentage is the starting point. You do not need a perfect measurement. A DEXA scan is most accurate if you have access to one. Skinfold callipers with a trained assessor are reliable. The Naval body fat formula is a reasonable estimate from measurements alone.

As a working guide for women:
- Above 27 to 28% body fat: Category 3
- Between 20 and 27%: Category 2
- Below 17 to 18%: Category 1

For men:
- Above 20 to 22%: Category 3
- Between 13 and 20%: Category 2
- Below 10 to 12%: Category 1

These ranges are approximate. Athletic history, training age, and individual hormonal sensitivity all play a role. A woman who has been lean and competitive for years may show Category 1 responses at slightly higher body fat than someone who recently lost a significant amount of weight. The markers to watch for are always more reliable than the number on its own: training performance, recovery quality, menstrual cycle regularity, and how the body actually responds to the deficit you are running.

---

WHERE TO GO FROM HERE

If you are Category 3 and trying to use Category 1 caution, push a bit harder. Your body can handle it and will respond well.

If you are Category 1 and you have been eating in a large deficit, training twice a day, or progressively losing performance and cycle regularity, the approach needs to change. The answer is more food and less volume, not more restriction.

If you are Category 2, the sweet spot is precision. Not aggressive, not timid. The right deficit, the right protein, the right training load, and enough patience to let the process work.

Understanding which category you are in is the first question any honest fat-loss coaching conversation should start with. Everything else follows from there.
