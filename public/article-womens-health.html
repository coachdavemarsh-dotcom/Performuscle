WOMEN'S HEALTH

Women's Physiology
and Training

Women are not small men. Your cycle, your hormones, and your fat physiology work differently. Here is what that actually means for how you should train and eat.

By Coach Dave Marsh 15 min read Women's Health | Performance

I have worked with a lot of women over the years. And one pattern shows up constantly: women who are working hard, eating reasonably well, doing most things right, but getting results that do not match their effort. Their progress stalls. Their energy fluctuates wildly across the month. They lose fat at a pace that frustrates them. Their body seems to work against them in ways they cannot explain.

Most of the time, the answer is not effort. The answer is information. The information they were never given.

The fitness industry has been built largely on research done on men, by men, applied to everyone. That is starting to change, but slowly. The reality is that women's physiology differs from men's in ways that go far beyond simply having a smaller body. The menstrual cycle creates a constantly shifting hormonal environment that touches almost every aspect of how the body performs, burns fuel, stores fat, builds muscle, manages hunger, and responds to stress. Ignoring that is like navigating with a map drawn for a different country.

This article is my attempt to give you an accurate map.

During a normal menstrual cycle, a woman's insulin sensitivity, fuel usage, metabolic rate, hunger, fatigue, strength, coordination, injury risk, and body weight can all shift significantly. Men are essentially the same every day. The comparison is not apples and oranges. It is apples and something else entirely.

THE TWO HALVES OF YOUR CYCLE

Everything starts here. Your monthly cycle, averaging around 28 days, splits into two distinct phases that create very different physiological environments. Understanding both is the foundation for understanding your body.

DAYS 1 TO 14 (APPROX)
Follicular Phase
  • Estrogen dominates
  • High insulin sensitivity
  • Better fat burning during exercise
  • Faster muscle repair and lower muscle damage
  • Reduced inflammation after hard sessions
  • Hunger closer to normal baseline
  • Better coordination and power output
  • Ideal for pushing hardest training
DAYS 15 TO 28 (APPROX)
Luteal Phase
  • Progesterone dominates
  • Reduced insulin sensitivity
  • Higher resting core temperature
  • Drives hunger up 90 to 500 cal/day
  • Activates fat-storage enzymes in lower body
  • Blocks many of estrogen's positive effects
  • Coordination and performance often dip
  • Better tolerated with lighter, skill-based work

Why This Matters For Training

Estrogen is genuinely a performance-friendly hormone. It improves how well your muscles use fuel, limits the damage done by intense training, speeds repair between sessions, and directly supports muscle remodeling. The follicular phase, when estrogen is dominant, is where women tend to make their best strength and hypertrophy gains. Loading your hardest training into this window is not just theory. There is good evidence supporting it.

Progesterone is essentially the opposite. It works against you in the gym. The elevated core temperature alone has measurable effects on performance and perceived effort. The insulin resistance means your muscles are less efficient at using carbohydrate for fuel. The increased hunger, often 90 to 500 calories per day above normal, creates a daily battle to maintain the intake that supports your goals. Women who wonder why they seem to eat everything in sight for two weeks every month now have a physiological explanation.

And then there is body weight. Women can legitimately gain 5 to 10 pounds of scale weight from water retention alone across the cycle, driven by hormonal fluctuations and increases in progesterone-driven fluid retention in the late luteal phase. This weight is not fat, but it is real, and it drives many women to make panicked diet decisions in the worst possible window.

HOW WOMEN STORE AND LOSE FAT DIFFERENTLY

One of the most practically important things I can tell a female client is this: women's fat physiology is not just a scaled-down version of men's. It is fundamentally different in ways that explain a lot of the frustration women experience when their approach to fat loss does not work as expected.

Lower Body Fat Is Not a Willpower Problem

Fat cells in different parts of the body have different receptor ratios. The two key receptors here are beta-2, which promotes fat release when stimulated, and alpha-2, which blocks fat release when stimulated. Lower body fat in women, particularly hip, thigh, and glute fat, has a much higher ratio of alpha-2 to beta-2 receptors than upper body fat. This means the body's natural fat-mobilizing signals, particularly adrenaline during exercise and fasting, are actively blocked in those specific areas.

Estrogen directly amplifies this effect by upregulating the alpha-2 receptors in lower body fat tissue and activating the fat-storage enzymes (particularly lipoprotein lipase) in those same areas. Progesterone layers on top of that in the luteal phase, further promoting storage. The result is a fat depot that is biologically designed to store readily and release reluctantly. This is not a defect. It is an evolutionary feature, a caloric reserve for pregnancy and lactation. But understanding it means stopping the self-blame when lower body fat refuses to respond the way upper body fat does.

Women Lose Less Muscle While Dieting

This is one of the areas where women actually have an advantage over men, though it rarely feels that way. When women diet, they lose a smaller proportion of lean mass relative to total weight lost. Some research suggests only about one-third of the weight women lose while dieting is lean mass, compared to higher proportions in men. Estrogen has a direct protective effect on muscle tissue during caloric restriction, partially preserving it even in a deficit.

The downside of this is that women cannot create the same absolute caloric deficits as men and still maintain performance. Men have more absolute muscle mass, and muscle is the primary driver of metabolic rate. A woman dieting with a large deficit will experience a more severe relative metabolic adaptation than a man of the same bodyweight. The sustainable deficit for most women, especially athletic women, is smaller than it is for men, which means fat loss takes longer. That is not a failure. That is just the math.

HORMONAL MODIFIERS: WHEN THE CYCLE IS NOT NORMAL

Not every woman experiences a textbook menstrual cycle, and several conditions can significantly alter the hormonal environment in ways that compound the already complex picture above.

PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome)

PCOS is the most common hormonal condition in women of reproductive age, affecting around 10 to 15% of the population. The defining feature from a training perspective is elevated androgens, including testosterone, combined with a high degree of insulin resistance. PCOS women often carry more visceral fat than women with the same body fat percentage and a normal cycle. The higher insulin resistance makes carbohydrate management significantly more important than in women without PCOS. A lower carbohydrate, higher fat dietary approach often produces better results, and strength training is particularly beneficial because it improves insulin sensitivity directly.

Hormonal Birth Control

Because this topic deserves its own deep dive, I have covered it in a separate article. The short version: hormonal birth control suppresses natural estrogen and progesterone production, reduces testosterone by roughly 50% on average, and increases SHBG, which further lowers free testosterone available to muscle tissue. The type of birth control matters enormously, and the hormonal IUD stands apart as the option with the least systemic impact on training.

Perimenopause and Menopause

As estrogen declines in the years approaching menopause, the physiological advantages of the follicular phase gradually disappear. Insulin sensitivity worsens. Fat distribution shifts toward the abdomen. Muscle loss accelerates. Bone density decreases. Strength training becomes even more critical, not optional, at this stage of life. Women in perimenopause who stop lifting or substantially reduce their training often look back on that decision as the worst thing they did for their long-term health and physique.

ENERGY AVAILABILITY AND MENSTRUAL HEALTH

This is the section of this article I want every female athlete to read twice. Energy availability is one of the most important concepts in women's training, and it is one of the least understood outside of elite sport medicine.

Energy availability (EA) refers to the amount of dietary energy left over after accounting for exercise energy expenditure, expressed relative to lean body mass. Put simply: how much fuel your body has available for everything it needs to do beyond surviving your training sessions.

The critical threshold is approximately 30 calories per kilogram of lean body mass per day (around 13.6 cal per pound of LBM). Drop consistently below this level and the body begins shutting down non-essential systems to conserve energy. The menstrual cycle is one of the first things to go.

Menstrual Dysfunction Is a Warning Sign, Not a Goal

Losing your period is not a normal adaptation to hard training. It is your body telling you that it does not have enough energy to keep functioning normally. Amenorrhea, the loss of the menstrual cycle, is driven by chronically low energy availability, not by low body fat percentage specifically. Women with perfectly normal body fat levels can become amenorrheic if they are expending more energy in training than they are consistently replacing through food.

The consequences of amenorrhea extend far beyond fertility. The drop in estrogen that accompanies it directly accelerates bone density loss. Multiple studies have found that amenorrheic athletes can have the bone density of women 20 to 30 years older than their chronological age. This is a long-term structural problem that is not easily reversed. And importantly, going on hormonal birth control does not fix it. Birth control will restore the appearance of a cycle, but it does not restore the estrogen levels that protect bone density when those levels are suppressed by inadequate energy availability.

What Causes Low Energy Availability

In most cases it is not deliberate starvation. The more common scenario is a combination of factors that individually seem manageable but together cross the threshold. Training volume increases, dietary intake does not increase proportionally, and the gap widens over months. Sometimes it starts with intentional dieting that goes further or longer than planned. Sometimes it is simply not knowing how much food high training loads actually require.

The warning signs to watch for include: irregular or absent periods, stress fractures, persistent fatigue that does not respond to rest, recurrent illness, difficulty maintaining or gaining muscle despite consistent training, and mood changes including increased irritability and difficulty concentrating.

STRESS, CORTISOL, AND THE HORMONAL CASCADE

Stress is a training variable. Most coaches treat it as a lifestyle issue. But the physiological effects of chronic psychological stress on a woman's hormonal environment are significant enough that they need to be actively managed as part of any serious training plan.

Women and men respond to stress differently at the hormonal level. Women tend to release cortisol more rapidly in response to a stressor, reach higher peak cortisol levels, and clear cortisol more slowly than men. The practical consequence is that women are more susceptible to the chronic cortisol elevation that comes from persistent life or training stress, and the downstream effects are more pronounced.

What Chronic High Cortisol Does

Cortisol is not inherently bad. It is a necessary stress hormone with important functions. But chronic elevation creates a cascade of problems for women in training:

  • Water retention: Chronically elevated cortisol causes the kidneys to retain sodium and water. This shows up as persistent puffiness that does not respond to diet changes and can mask fat loss completely on the scale for weeks at a time.
  • Insulin resistance: Cortisol antagonizes insulin, increasing blood glucose and making fat storage more likely, particularly in the abdomen.
  • Hormonal disruption: High cortisol suppresses GnRH, the signaling hormone that drives the menstrual cycle. This can contribute to irregular cycles even when energy availability is adequate.
  • Muscle breakdown: Cortisol is catabolic. Chronically elevated levels increase protein breakdown and limit muscle building even when training and nutrition are otherwise in order.
  • Sleep disruption: Elevated evening cortisol disrupts sleep quality, which then further elevates the next day's cortisol. A self-reinforcing cycle.

The combined effect of chronic training stress plus chronic life stress plus caloric restriction can push a woman into a hormonal state where fat loss effectively stops, despite a continued caloric deficit on paper. The body is in a state of perceived emergency and acts accordingly.

Recognising the Pattern

If you are doing everything right on paper, your training is consistent, your nutrition is tracked, your deficit is reasonable, but your weight has been stuck for weeks and you feel worse than when you started, stress and cortisol are high on my list of culprits. Particularly if you are also experiencing persistent water retention, disrupted sleep, increased anxiety or irritability, and a suppressed or irregular menstrual cycle.

PRACTICAL STRATEGIES FOR WOMEN

Train According to Your Cycle

You do not need to track your hormones with blood draws to use your cycle intelligently. The basic framework is straightforward. In your follicular phase, push your hardest sessions. This is when you can handle the most volume, the most intensity, and when you will make the most progress. If you are going to test your maxes, attempt a personal best, or add a training day, do it here.

In your luteal phase, the goal shifts. Maintain the training stimulus without trying to force progress. Keep intensity reasonable, manage volume, and accept that some sessions will feel harder than they should. The performance dip is real and physiologically explained. Fighting it aggressively tends to increase injury risk and cortisol output without producing better results.

Start Your Diet in the Follicular Phase

If you are beginning a deliberate fat loss phase, starting in the follicular phase gives you a meaningful advantage. Insulin sensitivity is higher, fat oxidation is better, and hunger is closer to baseline. Beginning a diet during the luteal phase, when hunger is elevated by hundreds of calories and insulin resistance is higher, sets you up for a much harder first two weeks and poorer initial adherence. Timing the start of your diet is a free performance advantage.

Eat More in the Luteal Phase

Across the luteal phase, your resting metabolic rate genuinely increases slightly and your body is dealing with greater demands on its energy systems. Fighting the hunger that comes with this by strictly maintaining a fixed calorie intake rarely works well. A more effective approach is a modest calorie increase in the luteal phase, sometimes 100 to 200 calories above what you eat in the follicular phase, which helps adherence across the full month without meaningfully changing fat loss over time.

Slow Fat Loss Beats Fast Fat Loss

Research is relatively clear that slower fat loss rates produce proportionally more actual fat lost and proportionally less lean mass lost than aggressive crash diets. For women specifically, this matters more than in men because the sustainable deficit is smaller to begin with, and because aggressive restriction is more likely to push energy availability below the critical threshold that triggers hormonal disruption. A monthly fat loss of 0.5 to 1% of body weight is almost always superior to trying to lose faster.

Restore Energy Availability First

If you are experiencing menstrual cycle disruption, persistent fatigue, or recurrent injury, the first priority is restoring energy availability before addressing anything else. The recommended approach is adding approximately 250 to 360 calories per day to current intake and simultaneously reducing training by one session per week. This sounds counterintuitive when the goal is fat loss, but a body in hormonal distress is not a body that is going to produce results. Fixing the foundation comes first.

Manage Stress as a Training Variable

Sleep, psychological stress, and training load all compete for the same recovery resources. When total life stress is high, the amount of training a woman can productively absorb goes down. This is not weakness. It is physiology. Trying to maintain full training volume during periods of extreme life stress often produces worse results than backing off slightly and protecting recovery quality.

The version of you that trains at 90% of your maximum but sleeps well, manages stress, eats adequately, and maintains a consistent cycle will outperform the version that trains at 110% while running on cortisol and willpower. Every time.

PUTTING IT TOGETHER

Women's physiology is genuinely more complex to work with than men's. Not because women are fragile, but because there are more variables in play. The monthly hormonal cycle creates a moving target that requires a more nuanced approach than a fixed, unchanging plan.

But that complexity also means there are more levers to pull. Understanding your cycle gives you information most people, including most coaches, never think to use. Knowing when to push, when to back off, how to time your nutrition, and how to read the signals your body is sending turns what felt like a liability into a genuine tool.

The goal of this article is not to make things feel more complicated. It is to give you enough context to make your current approach smarter without changing everything at once. Pick one thing from what you have read, the most relevant one to where you are right now, and apply it. Build from there.
